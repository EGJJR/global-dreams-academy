Now that we know who is on the court, we

points from one flat surface to another.
You know this idea from document
scanning apps. You take a photo at an
angle. The perspective distorts the
page, but the app turns it into clean
top-down view. We'll use the same idea
here, but instead of working with
documents, we'll work with basketball
court. A homography defines a
mathematical relationship between two
planes. One of these planes can be the
camera view of the court and the other
can be the top-down layout of the
basketball floor. Using this
relationship, we can transform any point
from one plane to its corresponding
location on the other. This
transformation is represented by a 3x3
matrix, often called the homography
matrix.
To calculate it, we need at least four
pairs of corresponding points between
the two planes. Once computed, the
matrix allows us to project any pixel
from the camera perspective onto the
flat top- down map of the court. But
there's a catch. Basketball cameras

visible part of the court and reference
point change. Marking them manually

automatically detect them on every

defined by an ID and its XY coordinates.
Think pose estimation, but the skeleton
is the court layout.
